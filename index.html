I'm a media ecologist. My main questions are: (1) Why don't we experience conviviality and thoughtfulness as often as we'd like? and (2) Could changes to the internet or to capitalism help? I used to think these issues were about coordination costs, resource discovery, or the redesign of social organizations. My perspective now is that humans need collective wisdom in order to make choices--especially choices that lead to conviviality and thoughtfulness--and our society is failing at providing the necessary collective wisdom. Our systems of communication, as they've scaled up, have become bad at circulating retrospective, long-term accounts about what's been of lasting value to people like us. At the same time, the complex landscape of modern communication has disconnected two kinds of information we need to see together: information about what good we might do, and information about who we might do it with. This makes it easy to forget, as a society, what makes life happy and meaningful, and to forget as individuals how much social support we might have available if we try to do happy and meaningful things. The modern environment has been muting transmission of the information about what is possible and what is good to do.
